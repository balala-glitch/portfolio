ART

Art is one of the first languages humanity ever spoke. Art began long before cities, and books, and playwrights, and paintbrushes, and wood-mounted canvases. The first humans, with their first instances of art, spoke to something inside themselves: the awe of life, the fear of death, the rhythm of the hunt, the magic of existence. In those flickering torchlit caves, art was born — not for galleries, but for meaning.

Art is how we understand the world. Our brains crave patterns, shapes, stories. We see faces in clouds, animals in stars, and sometimes entire worlds in spilled ink. Art is not separate from us; it is us. A 5-year old’s wobbly bright sun at the corner of the page, random sketches of eyes and anime characters on the margins of the math notebook, carving your lover's name with a heart on a tree, the temporary drawings of sand on a beach, the permanent artworks done at the tattoo shop; these acts of leaving marks — as small as they may be — are defiance of meaninglessness.

In ancient times, art was ritual. Then it became storytelling. Then philosophy. Then rebellion. Now it's reflection, identity, protest, play. Art helps us grieve, dream, fight, remember. It records who we are. It helps us survive; in dark times, people still make songs, still paint, still write poems. The way we arrange our lives — the colours we wear, the food we cook, the way we decorate our rooms, the way we tell stories about ourselves — this is everyday art. A sunrise seen through half-closed eyes. The way wind moves grass. The quiet geometry of city streets. The harmony of the countryside.

Life is, at its very core, the most fundamental piece of art.
